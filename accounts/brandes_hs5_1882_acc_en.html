Publication History of Main Currents

Main Currents in Nineteenth Century Literature was published between 1872 and 1890 in six volumes, after which it was reissued in a series of new editions, the last with his own revisions appearing in 1923-24. By then the sum total of each individual volume of the work had reached 15,000 copies.

The first editions were published through Gyldendalske Boghandel (F. Hegel) in Copenhagen, which after a 1903 merger was called Gyldendalske Boghandel Nordisk Forlag, now with offices in Copenhagen and Christiana (Oslo), later also in London and Berlin. After Brandes’ death in 1927 sales stagnated, until in 1967 Jespersen & Pio arranged a new edition of the whole work with modernized orthography. Emigrant Literature, the first volume, was additionally reissued in 1971 and 1999.

The individual volumes were quickly translated into German and sold well. In 1880 Brandes switched from Duncker (later Barsdorf) to Veit & Co. This set off a complex legal conflict, which resulted in editions from both publishers being published alongside one another.

In addition to this, the entire work appeared in Russian, English, Japanese, Chinese, Yiddish, and Spanish translations; the Polish edition contained only the first five volumes. Translations of individual volumes also appeared Czech, Finnish and French.

Here follows an introductory discussion of the lectures on which the volumes were based, the handwritten manuscripts and the details of publication. Thereafter follows an account of the various Danish editions of Main Currents, followed by the German editions and finally the six individual volumes in Danish and translated editions. The translations into the various languages are arranged chronologically, and therefore vary from volume to volume.

a. Lectures

The background of the six volumes of Main Currents in Nineteenth Century Literature was the series of lectures Brandes delivered between 1871 and 1886 at Copenhagen University, at which however he did not manage to secure a permanent position. Brandes had earned a doctorate in 1870 with a dissertation on Hippolye Taine and French aesthetics, and could therefore, as privatdocent, deliver the lectures in exchange for entrance fees. During the years 1877-1883, when he was residing in Berlin, Brandes returned to Copenhagen to deliver shorter lecture series; after his return in 1883 he received financial support from private sources. This support, roughly equivalent to the salary of a professor, was conditional on his delivering public lectures gratis at the university. It was arranged that the first iterations of his lectures were delivered in exchange for admission fees, while free and public versions were then given several days later.

Some information on the precise dates of the lectures that formed the basis of the published volumes of Main Currents is summarized on the title pages of the individual volumes. This is further supplement by notices and advertisements in newspapers, but only rarely by the university catalog. In certain instances it has not been possible to determine a precis date for the conclusion of a series. It is similarly the case that lectures on themes found in the six volumes that Brandes delivered at locations other than Copenhagen University can only be dated and titled with a degree of uncertainty.

b. Manuscripts

Brandes’ manuscripts are preserved to a large extent, the majority in the Brandes Archive at the Royal Library, which was established in 1912 and later bolstered considerably with gifs from Brandes himself as well as, after 1927, from his family.

At the end of the 1930s, and again at the beginning of the 1970s, the lecturer Morten Borup reviewed the manuscript collection at the Brandes Archive. The research librarian Kirsten Weber later reviewed them again, producing a 46 page index in 1986, which is accessible at www.kb.dk/permalink/2006/manus/691/dan/. The information in the index is not always precise and demands critical attention; individual manuscripts, among them those of the six volumes of Main Currents, are chronologically misplaced.

Aside from the Brandes-Archive, a significant number of manuscripts are stored in other collections at the Royal Library: The Collin Collection (Coll.), the New Royal Collection (NKS) and Later Acquisitions (Acc.). There are two published summaries of the manuscripts: Lauritz Nielsen, Katalog over danske og norske Digteres Originalmanuskripter i Det kongelige Bibliotek, 1943, og Birgitte Possing og Bruno Svindborg (eds.), Det Kongelige Biblioteks Håndskriftafdeling. Erhvervelser 1924-1987, 1-2, 1995. In 1988 the Royal Library transitioned to electronic indexing of the material held by the hand written manuscript department.

Wherever there are manuscripts, the placement (call number), size and description are provided. The abbreviation kps. stands for capsule (and sheet).

c. Book Details

Information on publications dates and print runs (stock for sale + review and gratis copies) is taken from the Gyldendal archive at the Royal Library, in individual from the “Danish Book Registry.” Wherever it is noted, the six volumes of Main Currents up to 1899 were sold unbound with letterpress printed wrapping. Bound copies were thus all bound privately and the placement of the half title page and the table of contents caries from example to example.

Price information is taken from the weekly publication of the Danish Book Registry in the Danish Bookseller News. Denmark changed its monetary standard from the rigsdaler (96 skillings) to the krone (100 øre) in 1875.

The division of the six volumes varies. In general there are (unnumbered) chapters. Chapter titles are listed in the tables of contents; in most, but not all cases, chapter titles also appear at the top of each page. The chapters can be further divided into shorter sections, the titles of which can be indicated at the top of the page. All sections are indicated by a Roman number in a continuous series for the whole volume; this is not however the case with Emigrant Literature.

Page measurements are listed as broad and long and excluding pagination and page titles.

d. Collected Writings

Brandes’ Collected Writings, published between 1899 and 1902 in 12 volumes, were billed as the “Standard and Popular Edition” and sold by subscription, 89 installments in total. The installments were normally 80 pages, corresponding to five sheets and priced at 50 øre. Page measurement was 102 x 149 mm.

The print run for the first installment that kicked of the edition in March 1899 was 14,000, but eventually this stabilized at around 6000 copies. There were various publisher’s bindings. Volume 12 has an index of named persons, which covers the whole of Main Currents, contained in volumes four, five and six. Between 1903 and 1910 six supplemental volumes were added to the original 12.

In the course of preparing his Collected Writings, Brandes conducted an expansive revision of the material, for example replacing foreign words and expressions with Danish synonyms. At the same time the orthography was modernized, including the elimination of the plural verb forms. Brandes furthermore translated the majority of the foreign language citations, first and foremost those of poetry, into Danish. And finally Brandes revised the content of his works, typically by updating the material and making additions, which were often adapted from his own reviews and later articles.

The form Brandes gave to Main Currents in his Collected Writings was the basis for the English translation of 1901-1905, for the Japanese edition of 1915 as well as – via the English and Japanese translations, the (partial) Chinese edition of 1936.

Between March 1906 and April 1907, the fourth, fifth and sixth volumes of the Collected Works (containing Main Currents) were published without changes in a three volume popular edition, sold in 30 installments. The print run was 3000. Because of an error, this edition was designated as the “fifth edition.” This was was because Emigrant Literature was in its fifth edition, while the other five volumes were only in their fourth (at the time, no distinction was observed between “printing” and “edition”). The Collected Works as well as the popular edition of Main Currents were marketed in slightly varied publisher’s bindings. The front page was emblazoned with a round vignette of “Saint George and the Dragon,” the back with Saint George’s sword wound through a rose vine with thorns, the hilt forming the letters GB. A corresponding publisher’s binding was then used for other Brandes works.

e. The 1923-1924 Edition

In 1923 and 1924 Brandes once again revised Main Currents in Nineteenth Century Literature, adding among other things a new foreword. In relation to the revisions from the third to the fourth edition there a few changes. The poetry citations in volumes four, five and six are in most cases given in both the original language (English, French, German) as well as in Brandes’ Danish translations, thus combining the foreign language form of the early editions with the translations in the Collected Works.

The edition was published in six volumes with a print run of 3000, the first two volumes in 1923, the last four in 1924. Page measurement was 102 x 150 mm. Later the six volumes were bound in three publisher’s volumes. (Printed on the covers of the sheet copies was ’Københavns Universitet,’ on the title pages ’Kjøbenhavns Universitet’; “literatur” is spelled with a single “t.”

The edition did not sell well. Three years later Gyldendal thus repackaged it in the three volume format. In connection with Brandes’ 85th birthday in February 1927, the publisher reissued the edition in sheet format and with new covers; the first sheet was printed in 5000 copies, but sales did not take off, and after the issuing of still more sheets for subscription the project was abandoned in April 1928.

The 1923-1924 edition later became the basis for the edition of Main Currents issued by Jespersen and Pio in 1966-1967, but newly set and with modern orthography aside from the title page’s conspicuous combination of old and new. Each of the six volumes is supplied with illustrations, notes and a quite detailed index produced by the publisher’s chief, Ivar Jespersen (1904-1995).

Brandes’ translations of poetry from the Collected Writings are to a larger extent than in the edition of 1923-1924 reinserted and placed behind the foreign language citations. New, detailed page titles are further added. In the first volume of the edition (pp. 227-40), a reconstruction of Brandes’ introductory lecture of November 1871 by Henning Fenger (1921-1985), first written in 1956, is included.

f. German Editions

The first four volumes of Main Currents were quickly translated to German byAdolf Strodtmann (1829-1879), published as Die Hauptströmungen der Literatur des neunzehnten Jahrhunderts by Duncker of Berlin in 1872, 1873, 1874 and 1876.The publisher, led by Franz Duncker (1822-1888), was after a bankruptcy taken over by Hermann Barsdorff (18?-19?) and moved to Leipzig, although after 1900 it was relocated to Berlin (Charlottenburg).

In 1880 Brandes entered into an agreement with the publisher Veit & Co. of Leipzig (led by Hermann Credner, 1842-1924) for a new edition, now titled Die Literatur des neunzehnten Jahrhunderts in ihren Hauptströmungen dargestellt. The publisher bought the publication rights to the already published four translated volumes, but not the rights to Strodtmann’s translations. Since Denmark only signed the Berne Convention in 1903, Barsdorf could before then issue new printings and editions of Strodtmann’s translations without an agreement and without paying royalties to Brandes.

In 1886 Barsdorf issued a second printing, in 1892 a third with claiming to be the “Einzig autorisierte deutsche Ausgabe.” An edition of the first five volumes issued in 1894-1896 was marketed as “Vierte, vermehrte und mit einem Generalregister versehene Auflage.” These editions were as a rule provided with a double title page, the first with the title of the whole work and an indication of the print run, which only applied to the first four volumes and thus did not cover the last two. Furthermore, the fourth edition’s first and third volumes were dedicated to Paul Heyse, the second to Hippolyte Taine, and the fourth to Giuseppe Saredo.

The fifth edition was billed as “Jubilee edition” celebrating the 25th anniversary of the beginning of the work, and on page one contained the publisher Barsdorff’s foreword, dated November 1896:

Vorwort zur fünften, gänzlich umgearbeiteten und vermehrten Auflage. – Dieser fünften Auflage der Hauptströmungen sind die neuesten dänischen Originalausgaben und zwar […] zu Grunde gelegt worden, so dass des Werk jetzt in seiner neuen Gestalt den ”anfänglichen Hoffnungen und Absichten” seines Autors entspricht. Es erzählt ein Stück europäischer Seelengeschichte, indem es den Entwicklungsgang der Litteratur der ersten Hälfte des 19. Jahrhunderts darstellt. Ich habe diese fünfte Auflage Jubiläums-Ausgabe genannt, weil bei Beginn ihrer Drucklegung – im November 1896 – gerade fünfundzwanzig Jahre verflossen waren, seit die Hauptströmungen als Vorlesungen an der Kopenhagener Universität zum ersten Male gehalten wurden. Welches Aufsehen sie damals erregten, welche Begeisterung sie hervorriefen, ist von dem trefflichen Adolf Strodtmann, ihrem ersten Uebersetzer, in seiner klassischen Einleitung zum ersten Bande geschildert worden. Der Geist absoluter Freiheit, der in allen sechs Bänden herrscht, wird, dessen bin ich gewiss, den ”Hauptströmungen” weitere fünfundzwanzig Jahre in der Gunst der geistig Freigewordenen aller Länder sichern.

Leipzig, November 1896

Der Herausgeber

Thereafter (pp. III-XVIII) followed Strodtmann’s foreword to the first four volumes. The general index from the fourth printing was expanded to include the fifth volume, while the sixth had its own index.

In 1900 Barsdorf published Brandes’ Ausgewählte Schriften in nine volumes, of which three through eight contained Hauptströmungen. There was still another edition in 1906, designated the 10th and collecting all six volumes into two. The publisher further arranged for editions of parts of the individual volumes; these are not covered in this review.

It is evident that Barsdorf’s editions did not incorporate the revisions Brandes made to the Danish editions, so that the content of the various printings is not consistent. Since no royalties were paid to Brandes, the books could be sold at lower price than Veit & Co.’s editions.

Brandes’ agreement with Veit & Co. stipulated that new, revised editions of the first four volumes should be prepared in 1882. Only the fifth volume was newly issued in 1883. When Veit and Co. issued the second volume in the new series in 1887, Barsdorff, who had by then taken over Duncker, filed suit, because the first and second volumes relied excessively on Strodtmann’s translations. Brandes defended himself and the publisher with an account addressed to Hermann Credner and dated June 22nd, 1887. This was then published in Danish in Politiken on July 4th under the title “A Letter from Dr. G. Brandes.” After a series of hearings in numerous courts, the supreme court issued a ruling in favor of Barsdorf in 1889, and all remaining copies were confiscated.

Who it was who translated the new parts of the second volume remains unknown. The volumes had to be reconstituted according to a reworking by Brandes corresponding to the Danish editions of the 1890s. Hence parts of volume two and the whole of three and four were (anonymously) translated between 1898 and 1901 by Mathilde Prager (1844-1921), who for many years, generally under the pseudonym Erich Holm, translated and introduced Nordic authors such as Jonas Lie, August Strindberg, Henrik Ibsen, Georg Brandes and Jeppe Aakjær. For more on this, see Karin Bang: Mathilde Prager. Tolk for tidens store mænd, 2004; German edition: Stimme der grossen ihrer Zeit, 2006; Karin Bang: “Mathilde Prager und Georg Brandes,” in Text & Kontext, 26.1, 2004, pp. 119-134).

Barsdorf saw to the new translations of the final two volumes, the fifth in 1886 in a translation by W. Rudow, the sixth only in 1896 and translated by A. v. d. Linden, because a new rule protected the translation published by Veit & Co. in 1891.

After Denmark signed the Berne Convention, peace reigned in the conflict. Reiss Verlag of Berlin issued in 1923-1924 in three volumes Hauptströmungen der Literatur des neunzehnten Jahrhunderts. Vom Verfasser neu bearbeitete endgültige Ausgabe. (Unter Zugrundelegung der Übertragung von Adolf Strodtmann nach der Neubearbeitung des Verfassers übersetzt von Ernst Richard Eckert. The edition contained a combined name and theme index. The first volume was issued in a third printing the same year, while the two others appeared in a second printing.

The manuscripts for the German editions are indexed, but not the translations of Brandes’ published preliminary studies which encompassed a number of languages, especially German and Russian.

The Romantic School in France (1882)

a. Book Description

The Romantic School in France was published in Copenhagen on Decmber 12th, 1882. It was priced at 7.50 kroner.

Print run is unknown, but likely 1250 + 75 copies.

Hegel and Son). | Græbe’s Book Printing. | 1882. [4] mottoes of Th. de Banville and Schiller. [5]-593 text. [594] blank [595] Table of Contents. [596] Corrections

Page measurements: 77 x 129 mm.

The text is set with antiqua, display settings with italics. Citations are set with a smaller type size. The 24 (unnumbered) chapters in the Table of Contents are divided into 35 sections, designated with Roman numerals I-XXXV. The left pages have the page title The Romantic School in France, while the chapter titles serve as the page titles for the right pages. Supplemental corrections are found in Men and Works, 1883, p. 531.

The lectures were held at Copenhagen University in the form of two short installments: three lectures on September 27th, October 1st and October 5th, 1877 (Romantism in France) and eight between November 3rd and 27th, 1879 (on French literature from 1830-1848).

Because of an error the edition was designated as the Fifth Edition. It was published in installments between December 1906 and May 1907. The edition can be found in publisher’s binding.
