1. Introduction

1.1. Plan of the Edition

The edition of Georg Brandes' Main Currents in Nineteenth Century Literature (1872-1890) is a critical textual and annotated digital edition.

The primary audience of the edition is academic, that is to say researchers, students and those particularly interested in the material, but through the medium of electronic publication we hope to reach a broader readership, which by virtue of the English version of the site, thus becomes international.

The first editions of the six volumes of Brandes’ work serve as the textual basis, and the edition is supplied with a selective variant apparatus that illuminates the changes Brandes made in later editions. Added to this are detailed research-based introductions to the individual volumes of Main Currents, indexes of persons, works, literary characters and places, linking to the DSL’s dictionaries, search functions, a timelineof Brandes’ life and international activities, and various resource texts: German, English and French translations of the work as well as Brandes’ Explanation and Defense (1872) and autobiographical Memoirs 1-3 (1905-1908).

Also on the domain georgbrandes.dk is an exploration of Main Currents as a comparative literary and cultural critical event, which has been carried out within the parameters of the research project. The project’s experiments with digital methods of textual analysis are also documented.

The chief editor of the edition and website has been Jens Bjerring-Hansen (University of Copenhagen). See full list of staff, contributors and collaborators here.

2. Outline of the Material in the Edition

2.1. Texts

The edition consists of three groups of texts, including the Danish original edition of HS 1-6 and two types of supplementary resource texts, translations of HS to German, English and French and autobiographical writings

Georg Brandes’ Main Currents in Nineteenth Century Literature was published in Copenhagen in six volumes between 1872 and 1890, through the publisher Gyldendal. Each of the six volumes was further published in numerous new editions and translations during the lifetime of the author. The author’s manuscripts are only partially preserved for posterity.

  • vol. 1. Emigrantlitteraturen [Emigrant Literature]. 1872. (Includes the “Indledning” / “Introduction” to the whole work)
  • vol. 2. Den romantiske Skole i Tydskland [The Romantic School in Germany]. 1873
  • vol. 3. Reactionen i Frankrig [The Reaction in France]. 1874
  • vol. 4. Naturalismen i England [Naturalism in England]. 1875
  • vol. 5. Den romantiske Skole i Frankrig [The Romantic School in France]. 1882
  • vol. 6. Det unge Tyskland [Young Germany]. 1890

Translations of HS in German, English and French:

  • Die Hauptströmungen der Literatur des neunzehnten Jahrhunderts. 6 vols., Berlin 1872-87 (vols. 1-5) and Leipzig 1891 (vol. 6).
  • Main Currents in Nineteenth Century Literature, vol. I-VI, London/New York 1901-05.
  • Les grands courants littéraires au XIXe siècle. L'école romantique en France, Paris 1902 (only vol. 5).

The project also includes:

  • Forklaring og Forsvar. En Anti-Kritik [Explanation and Defense. An Anti-Critique]. Gyldendal 1872.
  • Levned [Memoirs] in three volumes. The original text consists of:

      • 1. Levned. Barndom og første Ungdom [Memoirs. Childhood and Early Youth] Gyldendal 1905.

      2. Levned. Et Tiaar [Memoirs: A Decade]. Gyldendal 1907.

      3. Levned. Snevringer og Horizonter [Memoirs: Narrow Passages and Horizons.] Gyldendal 1908.

2.2. Scale of the Edition

The edition is digital, but its scale can be grasped by considering the published books: 10, 232 book pages of text in all (with relatively compact pages of circa 1200-1500 characters with spacing).

  • HS 1-6 (2720 book pages in total)
  • HS translations to German (2850 pages), English (2.900 pages), French (500 pages)
  • Memoirs 1-3 (1180 pages) and Explanation and Defense (72 pages)

To this is added the secondary material of the editions: introductions to the individual texts, critical textual notes, historical and verbal commentaries, critical apparatus, publication history, user guides etc.

2.3. Organization and Specification of the Philological Work

The philological work was performed by specialists at the DSL. This work involves:

  • Selection of textual basis,
  • collation of texts,
  • preparation of text (including emendation, that is correction of errors in the original text, for example typographical and typesetting errors),
  • critical textual notes (with explanations for the corrections that the editors have made to the original text),
  • commentaries (with explanations of instances of difficult vocabulary),
  • indexing and establishing of databases on persons, works, places and literary characters,
  • preparation of (selective) critical apparatuses (with documentation of the most important changes made to HS in later editions from the author’s lifetime),
  • accounts of publication history (with accounts of the relevant manuscripts and editions, together with bibliographic descriptions of the editions that function as the foundation of the new edition, as well as
  • introductions (on the background of the work, its genesis and afterlife) written by an external research group in dialogue with the DSL editors.

However, not all of the texts receive identical treatment:

  • The translations of HS are not reproduced with textual criticism, but only as proofread OCR-scanner text. They are not commented upon and are accompanied only by brief introductions (by the DSL editors).
  • The biographical resource texts are reproduced with textual criticism, but in contrast to the Danish HS editions are not commented upon. They are accompanied by brief introductions.

3. Coding, Markup and Publication

3.1. Coding and Markup

The project follows the DSL’s coding manual, developed by Thomas Hansen in dialogue with digital publication projects at the DSL. The manual is based on the latest TEI standard (P5). The manual is adapted to the particular demands of the DigHo. In general, the Brandes texts are simple in structure, without many content elements. However, some of the IT solutions to philological problems deserve special consideration. This is true for the critical apparatus (see section 6) as well as the digital indexes (see section 7.2).

3.2. Publicering

The text is digitally published on DigHo’s domain, www.georgbrandes.dk (see section 10).

4. Selection of Source Text

The basis of the edition is the first published version of Georg Brandes’ books (first editions). The Danish texts are reproduced in facsimile (that is with digital image scanning), with the possibility of parallel viewing with the electronic text.

The selection of the first edition as textual basis– and the textual-sociological point of departure – is in line with current (new) philological author editions in Scandinavia (for example, Ludvig Holberg’s Writings, Grundtvig’s works, Henrik Ibsen’s Writings and the Selma Lagerlöf Archive). In this sense the selection is a natural extension of the project’s overriding sociological interest in Main Currents as a public event and cultural critical intervention. It was the first edition with which the public made acquaintance, and which immediately launched a new epoch, provoking jubilation and fury. The attention to the historical re-contextualization of the work weighs more heavily than an evaluation of its quality, which would redound to the advantage of the later, revised editions.#

# The other extreme – to look behind the first printings and thus base the edition on the lecture manuscripts or other handwritten material – is not materially possible. The manuscripts have only survived in part (see section 2 above). The selection of the textual basis with respect to HS is discussed by Per Dahl in his “Det kritiske tekstvalg. Problemer og perspektiver [Critical Text Selection. Problems and Perspectives] in Jørgen Hunosøe & Esther Kielberg (eds.): I tekstens tegn, 1994, pp. 96-124, which contains a pro and con discussion of “first” editions versus “best texts” as textual basis, and by Johnny Kondrup in his Editionsfilologi, 2014, p. 112, which recommends first editions.

5. Text Preparation

5.1. General Principles

The texts are as a starting point prepared without modernization (that is with the preservation of the original orthography and punctuation) and without normalization (that is without resolving inconsistencies in the author’s orthography and punctuation). In the interest of readability, however, certain older or inconsistent norms of typography and punctuation – without influencing the meaning – are presented according to more modern and/or consistent principles. This harmonization applies to the following factors:

  • typography
    1. Spacing as a means of emphasis is presented with italics; the very few examples of italics (in HS, vol. 6) used as an emphatic marker or etymological signal are likewise reproduced with italics.
    2. The page transitions and line breaks of the original are not imitated, but the original pagination is noted.
    3. Page titles (continuous headers) and sheet signatures are not reproduced continuously (see however immediately below with respect to “chapter headings”).
    4. The typographical inventory of the original is documented in the digital facsimile.
  • quotation marks and technique
    1. The French quotation marks “«… »” are replaced with the Danish “» … «”.
    2. Inserted initial quotation marks for new sections within longer citations are eliminated.
    3. Missing quotation marks: the oscillating practice regarding the marking of the conclusion of a citation as well as, rarely, the beginning of one, is harmonized by the insertion of the missing quotation marks. Missing quotation marks are only supplied when clearly lacking. In cases in which there is doubt, the inserted quotation marks are accompanied by a critical textual note.
    4. When marking of a break in a citation is missing, for instance due to Brandes insertion of an inquit formula, the text is improved according to modern systematics, ‘… by the insertion of a concluding quotation mark before the break and a beginning quotation mark after the break.
  • titles of works
    1. The staggering practice of marking titles of works is harmonized, so that they are always given with quotation marks: “» … «” In the case of doubt regarding classification (for example “Werther” – book or character?) the uncertainty is noted in a comment.
  • chapter headings
    1. The chapters of Main Currents do not have chapter headings, only a laconic numbering. To assist the reader of DigHo, the topical page titles are inserted as headers for the individual chapters in brackets “ […] ” In the case of volumes 1 and 2, in which there are many page titles per chapter, the first is given as header. A note to the header explains the wording of the page title.

5.2. Emendation og tekstfejl

DigHo corrects textual errors, particularly typesetting and typological errors in the original text (that is the first edition). The edition employs Johnny Kondrup’s definition of a textual error for the emendation: “a textual error is a (historically understood) disruption of the meaning of the text within a closely determined context” (Editionsfilologi, 2014, pp. 152-53). The determination of what constitutes a given error is thus not made according to a modern framework, but from a grounded understanding of the point of view of Brandes’ era. Thus when an error is identified, it is corrected in order to reconstruct an understanding of the text corresponding to what the author and his contemporaries would have understood.

The edition recognizes three types of errors.

  1. Trivial errors of a typographical nature, for example damaged type, uneven type, unintended typographical space between letters and punctuation signs. Missing quotation marks also belong to this category, when the placement of the mark is evident. These are corrected without notation.
  2. Banal errors where the result is a word that cannot exist in the context, and where at the same time it is clear what should be corrected, for example accent inversion, letters out of place or obvious written or typesetting errors ( for example “hum” for “hun,” or, in a given syntactical context “kan” for “han.”) These are corrected without reference to other textual sources.
  3. Genuine errors of various kinds, see below. These are corrected after consultation and if possible with documentation from later editions of HS from Brandes’ lifetime.

5.2.1. Emenderingsprocessen

In emending the text, later editions from Brandes’ lifetime are consulted to assist the process of correcting the first edition. If a possibly erroneous wording has been corrected to something more appropriate in a later edition, then there is strong argument that there was indeed an error in the first edition, which is then corrected according to the wording of the later edition. However, the later editions of HS are rewritten to a large extent – especially for the SS [Collected Works] – and they are also harmonized and modernized, so that they cannot always be used as a basis for correction.

The manuscript material of HS can also be drawn into the emendation process. Genetic variants – that is changes made to a manuscript during the writing process – are identified in the critical textual notes when they factor in the resolution of a given problem in the first edition (manuscript variants are provided only in such cases, consequentially not in the form of variant notes – see section 6 for the variant apparatus).

In the instances in which no textual sources have an acceptable wording, an editorial correction (conjecture) is rendered.

In emending the text, Brandes’ own corrigenda pages have been taken into account. Lists of corrections to the first edition are found in the back of later volumes and in other books by Brandes from these decades. Likewise, Brandes’ own corrections in print-ready manuscripts and in copies of the printed editions of (for example, L. Zeuthen’s copy from the KB). All these author’s corrections are sources of corrections, but not proof of textual error.

For the presentation of the critical textual notes, see below, section 5.3.

5.2.2. Factual Errors (that is, wrong name, date, place etc.)

Main Currents was produced in haste, especially the first four volumes (1872-75). The edition documents this, but does not remedy it; corrections are not made even when Brandes is way off the mark. The errors are identified and clarified in the commentaries.

5.2.3. Citation Errors

Citation errors by Brandes are only corrected (from the original source) if the error renders the citation meaningless or gives it a meaning that is clearly in disagreement with the context.

Errors that Brandes reproduces from his original texts are normally not corrected. In special cases in which an error is significantly consequential for Brandes’ argumentation, it is supplied with a commentary.

Regarding citations from the original language: if Brandes cites the wording from, for example, a specific edition of Taine, it is reproduced unchanged. The inverse applies as well: if Brandes has changed the wording, the changed form is preserved – and if there are essential changes made to the wording, this is noted in a commentary (and not a critical textual note). Adjustments to orthography or the punctuation of the original sources in Brandes’ text are not documented.

Regarding citations in translation: if Brandes in translating his citations changes the meaning of the source text, a note is made in a commentary (and not a critical textual note).

In general, the attempt to identify and consult the exact sources from which Brandes cites is only made when there is suspicion of textual error or changes to the wording (of a citation in the original language), or a change to the meaning (of a citation in translation). What is reconconstructed is Brandes’ reading and literary horizons rather than his library and loan records (see section 7.1 on the identification of citations in the historical commentary).

On the harmonization in DigHo of Brandes’ inconsistent citation see above, section 5.1.

5.2.4 Spelling Errors (foreign language)

When Brandes (or the typesetter) makes in error in the reproduction of a foreign language citation or a proper name, for example inverting French accent marks (aigu and grave), it is corrected as a banal error with a laconic note.

5.2.5. Punctuation Errors

The general principles of correction also apply to punctuation. Regarding emendations without notation, see above, section 5.2.

5.3. Presentation of Critical Notes

Corrections to the base text are documented in critical textual notes. Critical textual considerations can be expressed in a commentary; see examples in sections 5.2.2 and 5.2.3. On the difficulties of documenting the correct alternatives by consulting in later editions of HS, see above, section 5.2.1.

5.3.1. Presentation of Standard Notes

In cases in which an error in the first edition is corrected with reference to a variant from one or more textual sources, these sources’ symbols are given immediately after the lemma sign: ]. On the material situation and the abbreviations used to refer to the individual sources, see above, section 2.2

Example: Dem] B C SS FU 1923-24, dem A.

Where there is no textual source available to reveal the correct text, an editorial correction (conjecture) is made.

Example: Dem] dem A B C ms, Deres SS FU 1923-24.

In many cases the wording has been changed in later editions to the detriment of the basis for comparison. This is noted in a critical note like this:

Example 1: De] de A, changed wording due to revision in B and later editions (Emigrant Literature, p. 123, l. 18)

or (as intermediate form)::

Example 2: Digterne] Digtene B, changed wording due to revision in C and later editions. (Naturalism in England, p. 440)

5.3.2. Notation of Banal Errors

In some cases the critical textual note can be rendered less comprehensive than the standard note. When it involves a banal error, see above, section 5.2, a variant from a later edition is not given. In such cases only the erroneous rendering of the original is mentioned in the critical note.

Example 1: Udvalg,] Udvalg. A (The Reaction in France, p. 28)

Example 2: hvor] kvor A (Explanation and Defense, p. 52)

Trivial errors are corrected without notation, see above, section 5.1.

6. Variant Apparatus

Georg Brandes’ texts, not least the Main Currents, are dynamic entities that change from edition to edition by virtue of the author’s revisions. This applies to style and orthography, but also to the substance. The author became wiser and better read with respect to his themes, just as he acquired new aesthetic and ideological impulses.

This variant apparatus is selective, in that a quantitatively defined criterion of essentiality has been adopted in order to determine which variants should be included: two lines of changed text in the form of excisions, additions or rewriting in relation to the first edition. This same pragmatic conception of variance is the basis of Jens Kr. Andersen’s and Chr. Jackson’s “Georg Brandes: Emigrantlitteraturen” (Danske Studier, 1971) and Per Dahl’s preliminary work on a bibliography of Brandes’ authorship. Variants have been identified with the assistance of both digital collation tools and analog control reading.

In the introductions to each volume of Main Currents a general characterization of the textual variance in the later editions is given along with examples of the variance that has not been included (i.e. shorter than two lines).

The notes on variance are both classified (as excisions, rewritings or adidtions) and supplied with summary headers, which clarify the character of the revised section, for example “On Byron’s Second Address to Parliament.”

That which is compared is in all instances the first and second editions of the individual volumes of Main Currents. In certain instances, a German edition was published between two Danish editions and constituted the actual basis of the changes to the second Danish edition. The German variants are not included in the apparatus. For publication history and translation history of the individual volumes, see the Notes on the Publication History, which also includes brief descriptions of the larger changes made to later editions of the individual volumes.

7. Commentaries

To date neither HS nor any other of Georg Brandes’ essential works has been published with commentary. In other words there is no direct predecessor on which to rely. The target audience for DigHo is ideally the interested reader; a primary readership is however academic readers. The considerations below on the level of the commentary are especially focused on the historical commentaries (section 7.1), since the verbal commentaries are in principle all encompassing qua links to the digital Ordbog over det danske Sprog [Dictionary of the Danish Language] (section 7.3).

7.1. Historical Commentaries

7.1.1. Comments and Indexes

The scope of the historical commentaries is limited by the indexes that are tied to persons, works, places and literary characters in HS, see below, section 7.2. When it is considered necessary for the reader’s understanding of the text, a supplementary commentary can be added to the prosaic index entries at the appropriate place.

7.1.2. Main Areas of the Historical Commentaries

Besides the areas of knowledge covered by the indexes, the commentaries illuminate – to the extent that it is deemed necessary for the understanding of the text – matters including:

  • historical facts (events, phenomena, concepts etc.)
  • contemporary debates
  • references to mythological and biblical narratives
  • proverbs
  • references or connections to other Brandes texts (including correspondence)
  • clarification of critical textual problems, see above, sections 5.2.2 and 5.2.3
  • citations (primarily from literary texts, cf. 7.1.3)
  • Brandes’ sources (in collaboration with the overlapping commentaries in the afterword, see section 7.1.4)
  • certain older words (which are not covered by Ordbog over det danske Sprog [Dictionary of the Danish Language], or that have changed meaning since the publication of the dictionary, see section 7.1.5)

    • 7.1.3. Citations

    Sources for Brandes’ employment of text written or spoken by others are identified as broadly as possible, though with the flexibility in the coverage of the commentaries that citations from handbooks, historical accounts and the like are only documented to a modest extent.

    Certain passages placed in quotation marks cannot be determined to contain external text, but must rather be regarded as Brandes’ dramatized paraphrases or exegeses. These are noted in a comment.

    Citations are identified by the original title (though in cases of variance, the universally accepted form is used, for example Romeo and Juliet rather than The Most Excellent and Lamentable Tragedy of Romeo and Juliet), year of publication of the first edition, the location of the cited material (identification of the chapter or book or act or scene or poem title), and the page number in reliable modern edition (when possible) or in certain cases the first or later edition (the edition is given in shortened form with a link to full bibliographic information).

    • Example 1 (Emigrant Literature, p. 24) Note text: “Cit. from J.L. Heibergs vaudeville King Solomon and Jørgen Hattemager, sc. 26, 1825 (Heiberg 1862, 2:161).”
    • The following information appears when the shortened bibliographic entry in parenthesis is clicked on or hovered over: J.L. Heiberg, Samlede poetiske Skrifter, vol. 1-9, København, 1862.

    • Example 2 (Emigrant Literature, p. 4): Note text: “The motto is taken from the novel Ardinghello und die glückseligen Inseln, 1787, by the German poet Wilhelm Heinse (Heinse 1787, 1:5).”
    • The following information appears when the shortened bibliographic entry in parenthesis is clicked on or hovered over “Wilhelm Heinse: Ardinghello und die glückseligen Inseln, vol. 1-2, Lemgo, 1787.”

    For citations in the original language, Brandes’ later Danish translations in SS is given in this form: “GB’s translation in SS [Collected Writings] (vol. X, p. Y): …”. When the citation has been removed in connection with a revision, no translation is given.

    For citations in Danish, the cited work is identified, but the original wording is not reproduced unless they disagree.

    Textual problems in citations and translations are not noted in the critical apparatus, but in the commentaries, see above, section 5.2.3. In general, it should be noted that the essential aim of the commentary is at this point to identify the sources, not to examine the usage of citations.

    7.1.4. Sources

    As far as possible – that is to the extent of the secondary literature and the editors’ knowledge – Brandes’ scientific and literary sources and models are identified. The central corpus of Brandes’ source material and his “recycling technique” are touched on in the introductions to the individual volumes of HS.

    7.1.5. Relation to Verbal Commentaries

    Words needed for the commentaries that do not appear in Ordbog over det danske Sprog [Dictionary of the Danish Language] (or Meyer’s Fremmedordbog [Foreign Words Dictionary], 1924), are explained. Words that have changed meaning or whose double meaning has been lost are marked to alert the reader. See section 7.3.

    7.1.6. Presentation of the Commentaries

    The commentaries are kept brief and concise. The frequency of the commentaries is high in relation to analog editions, since phenomena that demand repeated commentary – independent of the context – are only unexplained when they appear on the same (book) page.

    The following conventions are observed:

    • Lemmas are given in basic form with conjugation endings in parenthesis, for examples “Krigen(s)”
    • Lemmas are abridged by using three periods, for example “that time . . . with”]

    For the conventions regarding the presentation of work, year, date etc., see section 9.4 .

    7.1.7. Important Aids

    The commentaries make use of existing Brandes scholarship and philology. Important aids in the production of the commentaries are the monographs on young Brandes and his main work:

    • Henning Fenger: Georg Brandes’ Læreaar (1955) and Den unge Brandes (1957)
    • Bertil Nolin: Den gode europén. Studier i Georg Brandes’ idéutveckling 1871-1893 (1965)
    • Gunnar Ahlström: Georg Brandes’ Hovedstrømninger (1937)
    • Jørgen Knudsen's biography Georg Brandes 1-8 (1985-2004)

    In addition, the editions of correspondence, particularly:

    • Georg og Edvard Brandes. Brevveksling med nordiske Forfattere og Videnskabsmænd I-VIII, 1939-42. Eds. Morten Borup, Francis Bull & John Landquist
    • Correspondance de Georg Brandes I-III, 1952-66. Ed. Paul Krüger
    • Georg Brandes. Breve til Forældrene 1859-71 I-III, 1978. Ed. Morten Borup
    • Georg Brandes. Breve til Forældrene 1872-1904 I-III, 1994. Ed. Torben Nielsen

    Finally, we use have been made can make use of Paul Krüger’s unpublished index of persons for GB’s Memoirs 1-3 (Brandes Archive, AU).

    7.2. Indexes (persons, works, places, literary characters)

    Persons, works, places, and literary figures are not commented upon locally in the historical commentaries, unless in exceptional cases it is deemed necessary for the reader’s understanding of the text. Material regarding these thematic areas is instead presented in brief index entries of a generic character (so that the same information on a person, place etc. is applicable everywhere). The entries form the basis of the electronic index, which functions as an entry point to the texts.

    7.2.1. Persons

    are clarified with name, dates of birth and death, nationality, profession, and other general information.

    • Example: Emil Aarestrup (1800-1856), Danish poet and physician.

    7.2.1. Works

    are clarified with author name, work title (with series, if necessary), genre, year and other general information.

    • Example: Honoré de Balzac: Eugénie Grandet, 1833.

    7.2.1. Places

    are clarified with name (in a modern form as far as possible), political-geographic category (from the modern perspective), country, and any historical explanation needed.

    • Example: Prussia, historical kingdom in Germany (became a part of the German Imperial Reich in 1871)

    Continents and contemporary states are not indexed. .

    7.2.1. Literary Charcters

    are clarified with author, work, year, and explanatory information of a general nature (for example information on the cast of a drama.

    • Example: Allegro, Ludwig Tieck: Die verkehrte Welt, 1799 (personified musical category).

    7.3. Verbal Commentaries

    The verbal commentaries do not function in the traditional manner. On the dictionary platform of the Society for Danish Language and Literature a web service has been established that returns dictionary entries for direct viewing on the homepage. All the words in the text – aside from the indexed proper names: person, places, works, literary figures – are able to be looked up, and to the extent that the word appears in one of the dictionaries relevant for the period, the relevant information will be shown.

    Dictionary entries are first and foremost drawn from the digital edition of the most relevant historical dictionary of the period, Ordbog over det danske Sprog [Dictionary of the Danish Language], ODS. If the word does not appear in the ODS, an entry from Meyer’s Fremmedordbog [Foreign Word Dictionary], MF will appear, and if not there, from the standard dictionary of modern Danish, Den Danske Ordbog, DO.

    The linguistic technological work has consisted in the initial development (of a full form list based on ODS’s word descriptions, a web service for 19th century Danish, which recognizes known conjugated forms of words and the most common, systematic spelling variations of the period, and which can manage searches in the dictionaries in the inventory in the most expedient order, and which furthermore supports manually chosen exceptions for automatic searches), and later adjustments.

    Next, the philological work consisted of:

    1. Establishing dictionary links to the words that are either not automatically retrieved or where the automatic retrieval is not expedient (for example, Brandes using spelling form 'Eiendom' for 'ejendom');
    2. Identifying and marking “false friends” in Brandes’ texts, that is words that have changed meaning since ODS, for example “patetisk” or “lemfældig.” Also belonging to this category are words that have multiple meanings, but where the meaning relevant to the text has been lost, for example “naturalisme.” Such words are marked to make the reader aware of the historical meaning
    3. Supplying commentaries for instances in which the dictionaries are not sufficient, because Brandes’ usage of a word is wholly outside the inventory of the dictionaries, or its usage is not covered by the explanations of meanings.

    9. Text Introductions

    9.1. In General

    The introductions (or afterwords) are general commentaries on the individual volumes of HS. The afterwords provide a lead into the respective work, offering historical contextualization as well as analytic and interpretative perspectives on the work.

    9.2. Content, Composition and Demarcation

    In general, the introductions pay special attention to HS as an international project – sociologically, scientifically, aesthetically or ideologically – by accounting for Brandes’ strategies for internationalization, comparative methodology, European scope, world literary point of view, his international inheritance and debts or similar.

    The afterwords are uniformly constructed (aside from the general one on the textual history of HS). The main sections, which are disposed and filled out in various manners, and whose relative scope necessarily changes from text to text, are as follows:

    1. Introduction
    2. Background and Genesis
    3. [Free section]
    4. Reception and Afterlife
    5. Bibliography

    9.2.1. Introduction

    The introductions are capsule texts that point out the central problematics of the following sections and the context(s) in which Brandes’ text should be read.

    9.2.2. Background and Genesis

    This section on the background of the work and its genesis involves an account of Brandes’ situation and other activities around the time he held the lectures and the publication of the relevant volumes of HS. But other types of general background and concrete circumstances can also be included, of, for example intellectual, cultural and literary historical nature.

    9.2.3. Free Section

    After a brief sketch of the content, the author has been free to decide how the work shall be analyzed and interpreted, since the research project’s general situating of HS as an international phenomenon is woven into the context.

    9.2.4. Reception and Afterlife

    This section documents and illustrates the positions taken in the contemporary reception of Brandes’ work as well as the later reception – or the history of its impact in Denmark and abroad.

    9.2.5. Bibliography

    The editors provide individual accounts of the manuscripts and printings of the respective work (see above, section 8).
